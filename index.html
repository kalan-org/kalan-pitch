The Science

Built on 10 years of cognitive science R&D

Kalan's learning engine doesn't use generic AI content. Every interaction is governed by peer-reviewed principles of how humans — especially children — actually retain and apply knowledge.

🔁
Spacing Effect
Content revisited at optimal intervals moves knowledge into long-term memory. Children don't forget what they learn.
Ebbinghaus (1885)
🎯
Adaptive Difficulty
Each child progresses at their own pace. The system adjusts in real time — always challenging, never overwhelming.
Vygotsky ZPD (1978)
⚡
Immediate Feedback
Corrections and encouragements happen the moment an answer is given — replacing the absent teacher in a proven way.
Hattie et al. (2007)
🧠
Cognitive Load Theory
Content broken into optimal chunks prevents mental overload and maximises how much each session actually sticks.
Sweller et al. (2019)

The Product

Kiosk prototype — design principles

The kiosk is not a school replacement. It is a precision learning tool — short, high-intensity sessions of 20–40 minutes, 3–5x per week. No teacher required during sessions. A community facilitator is present for safety only.

☀️
Solar-autonomous
Panel tilted 15° for equatorial latitudes. Lithium battery backup. 8–12h daily autonomy. Zero grid dependency.
📱
4 touch tablets, 8"
Tempered glass. Ruggedised bezels. Screens at 75–90cm from floor — ergonomic for children ages 6–9 without a step.
🎧
Audio-first design
4 headphone jacks. Interface speaks before it writes. Essential for low-literacy learners and multilingual contexts.
⏱️
Short session model
Arrive → 3min diagnostic → 20min active learning → feedback → done. The kiosk design physically invites short sessions, not marathon use.
On the "6–8 hours" question: Kalan is not a babysitting device. Think physiotherapy vs. hospital stay — precision over duration. Spacing effect research shows 20-minute repeated sessions outperform multi-hour blocks for long-term retention in children (Ebbinghaus, 1885; Kornell & Bjork, 2008).

Sources & References

All figures are sourced and verifiable

Every statistic in this deck links to a primary source — UNESCO, World Bank, UNICEF, or peer-reviewed research. No secondary aggregators.

The Problem — Out-of-school crisis
251M
Children and youth out of school globally
UNESCO Global Education Monitoring Report, 2024. Over half in Sub-Saharan Africa. Out-of-school population reduced by only 1% in nearly 10 years.
→ unesco.org/gem-report
89%
Children in Sub-Saharan Africa cannot read a simple text by age 10
World Bank & UNICEF, State of Global Learning Poverty 2022 Update. Defined as "learning poverty" — unable to read and understand a simple text by end of primary school. The rate for all low- and middle-income countries is 70%.
→ worldbank.org — Learning Poverty
-25%
Projected drop in international aid to education, 2023–2027
UNESCO GEM Report analysis, 2025. A 12% decline was already observed in 2024. In low-income countries, aid represents an average of 17% of public education spending — in some cases up to half of national education budgets.
→ unesco.org/en/education-financing
15M
Additional teachers needed in Sub-Saharan Africa by 2030
UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS), 2023. Low-income countries need to increase primary school teachers by nearly 50% to meet SDG4 targets. This structural deficit makes autonomous learning solutions essential, not optional.
→ UNESCO Education Finance Gap Report
~47%
of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has no electricity access
Our World in Data, 2024 (sourced from IEA & World Bank). The absolute number without electricity has increased in SSA as population growth outpaces grid expansion — making solar-autonomous hardware a necessity, not a premium feature.
→ ourworldindata.org/energy-access
The Science — Cognitive research behind Kalan's pedagogy
1885
Spacing Effect — Ebbinghaus, H. (1885)
Über das Gedächtnis (Memory: A Contribution to Experimental Psychology). Foundational research establishing that distributed practice across time dramatically outperforms massed practice for long-term retention.
1978
Zone of Proximal Development — Vygotsky, L.S. (1978)
Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes. Harvard University Press. Basis for Kalan's adaptive difficulty engine — content is always calibrated just beyond the child's current level.
1988
Cognitive Load Theory — Sweller, J. (1988, updated 2019)
Sweller, J., van Merriënboer, J.J.G., & Paas, F. (2019). Cognitive Architecture and Instructional Design: 20 Years Later. Educational Psychology Review, 31(2), 261–292. Basis for Kalan's chunked content design.
2007
Feedback & Learning — Hattie, J. & Timperley, H. (2007)
The Power of Feedback. Review of Educational Research, 77(1), 81–112. Identified feedback as one of the highest-impact influences on student achievement (effect size d=0.73). Basis for Kalan's immediate response engine.
2008
Desirable Difficulties & Session Length — Kornell, N. & Bjork, R.A. (2008)
Learning Concepts and Categories: Is Spacing the "Enemy of Induction"? Psychological Science, 19(6), 585–592. Supports Kalan's 20–40 minute session model over extended daily use.
